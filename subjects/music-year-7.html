‚ùì Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Do students need prior musical experience or their own instrument?
A: No prior experience is needed. All instruments and equipment are provided in class, including keyboards, percussion instruments, and access to computers with Sibelius software. Students learn from the very beginning, making Year 7 Music accessible to everyone.
Q: How is Year 7 Music assessed throughout the year?
A: Assessment is varied and practical, including individual vocal and keyboard performances, small group percussion work on Samba drums, and composition submissions using digital software. Students are assessed on both technical proficiency and creative understanding, with opportunities to demonstrate progress in different musical areas throughout the year.
Q: What technology and software do students learn to use?
A: Students work with Sibelius, professional music notation software used by composers worldwide. They learn to input notes, create drum patterns, compose melodies, and produce complete musical pieces for various contexts including pop music, classical concerto, and contemporary game music. All software training is provided in class.
Q: How much practice or homework is expected each week?
A: Music homework typically involves 30-40 minutes per week, including practicing keyboard pieces learned in class, completing short composition exercises, or listening to set pieces and identifying musical elements. Most practical work happens during lessons, with homework reinforcing classroom learning and developing independent musical thinking.
Q: What opportunities are there for students who particularly enjoy music?
A: Students can join school ensembles, choirs, and music clubs. Peripatetic lessons are available for various instruments. High-achieving students are encouraged to participate in school performances, competitions, and can pursue formal music graded examinations. The Music department supports all students who wish to develop their musical talents beyond the classroom.

  • "What is a canon and how do you perform one correctly?" üí° A canon is when singers perform the same melody starting at different times, creating harmony. Success requires careful listening to maintain your part while others sing different sections, keeping steady tempo and accurate pitch throughout.
  • "How do you identify notes on the treble clef stave?" üí° The treble clef shows notes from E (bottom line) to F (top line). Lines spell EGBDF (Every Good Boy Deserves Football), and spaces spell FACE. Each position represents a specific pitch on the keyboard or voice.
  • "What is the difference between melody and harmony?" üí° Melody is the main tune you sing or play - the part you remember and hum. Harmony is additional notes sung or played at the same time that complement the melody, creating richer, fuller sound through different pitches working together.
  • "How do you play keyboard with both hands together?" üí° Start by practicing each hand separately until confident, then combine slowly. Keep proper hand position with curved fingers, use correct finger numbers, and practice coordination by playing simple patterns where both hands move together before attempting independent movement.
  • "What are the main musical elements we identify when listening?" üí° Key elements include: Pitch (high/low), Dynamics (loud/quiet), Tempo (fast/slow), Texture (thin/thick layers), Timbre (sound quality of instruments), Duration (long/short notes), and Structure (how the music is organized in sections).
  • "Why is proper breathing and posture important for singing?" üí° Good posture allows your lungs to fully expand for deep breathing, giving you more air to sustain notes. Proper breath control from your diaphragm produces stronger, steadier tone with better pitch accuracy and projection, preventing strain and allowing longer phrases.

  • "What is call and response and how is it used in Samba music?" üí° Call and response is when one person or group plays a rhythmic pattern (the call) and others answer with a different pattern (the response). In Samba, the lead drummer signals changes, and the ensemble responds together, creating dynamic conversation through rhythm.
  • "How do you create and maintain a polyrhythm?" üí° A polyrhythm is two or more different rhythms played simultaneously. To create one, establish a steady beat pattern, then layer a contrasting rhythm on top. Success requires concentration, listening carefully to others, and maintaining your own pattern independently without being distracted.
  • "What are the main sections of a pop song structure?" üí° Pop songs typically have: Introduction (sets the mood), Verse (tells the story, usually 2-3 verses), Chorus (catchy repeated section with main message), Bridge (contrasting section providing variety), and Outro (ending section). The pattern is often: Intro-Verse-Chorus-Verse-Chorus-Bridge-Chorus-Outro.
  • "How do you use Sibelius software to compose music?" üí° Sibelius allows you to input notes by clicking on the stave, typing with keyboard shortcuts, or using a MIDI keyboard. You can add dynamics, tempo markings, and articulations. The software plays back your composition so you can hear it immediately, allowing you to edit and refine your work.
  • "What rhythmic devices make drum patterns interesting?" üí° Effective devices include: Syncopation (accenting off-beats), Repetition (memorable patterns), Variation (changing patterns slightly), Fills (short flourishes between sections), Dynamic contrast (loud/quiet), and Rests (strategic silence creating tension and release).
  • "Why is improvisation important in music and how do you do it?" üí° Improvisation develops musical creativity, listening skills, and confidence. To improvise, start with a simple pattern you know well, then gradually add small changes - vary rhythm, change dynamics, or add decorations. Don't worry about mistakes; they often lead to interesting new ideas in the moment.

  • "What makes an effective melody memorable and interesting?" üí° Effective melodies balance repetition (making them memorable) with variety (keeping them interesting). They use clear contour (shape), logical phrase structure, strong rhythmic character, and a mix of steps and leaps. The best melodies tell a musical story with a clear beginning, middle, and end.
  • "How does theme and variation work in classical music?" üí° Theme and variation starts with a clear, simple melody (the theme). Composers then create variations by changing elements while keeping the theme recognizable: altering rhythm, adding ornaments, changing dynamics, switching major/minor, or adding harmony. Each variation explores the theme differently.
  • "What are the key features of minimalist music?" üí° Minimalism uses: Repetition (patterns repeated many times), Gradual change (small modifications over time), Ostinato (persistent repeated pattern), Layering (building texture), Limited material (few notes or rhythms), and Phasing (patterns shifting gradually against each other), creating hypnotic, meditative effects.
  • "Why is minimalism effective for computer game music?" üí° Game music must loop seamlessly for extended periods without becoming annoying. Minimalism's repetitive nature suits looping, gradual changes maintain interest without distraction from gameplay, and simple patterns require less computer memory. The hypnotic quality enhances immersion and focus.
  • "How do you develop a simple melodic motif into a complete composition?" üí° Start with a short, memorable motif (2-4 notes). Develop it by: Repeating at different pitches (sequence), Playing it backwards (retrograde), Upside-down (inversion), Longer/shorter notes (augmentation/diminution), Adding harmony, or Combining with new material. Build phrases into sections creating complete structure.
  • "What is the difference between a concerto and other classical forms?" üí° A concerto features a solo instrument (or small group) contrasted with full orchestra. It showcases the soloist's virtuosity through challenging passages, uses dialogue between solo and tutti (everyone), typically has three movements (fast-slow-fast), and includes a cadenza where the soloist improvises freely.
